What America Taught Me About Race

By Ifemelu • Philadelphia, PA • 5 min read

I arrived in America expecting to learn new customs, new foods, new classrooms, and new ways of speaking. I expected the cold. I expected homesickness. I even expected confusion. What I did not expect was to learn that I was Black.

In Nigeria, I was Nigerian. I was Igbo. I was a girl with opinions, with friends, with a mother who worried, and with a future that felt close enough to touch. But in America, before I could be any of those things, I became something else first. I became a category.

Race in America is not always shouted. Sometimes it is whispered through questions, assumptions, compliments, silences, and smiles that last a little too long.

People asked where I was from, and then where I was really from. They praised my English as if I had discovered it in my suitcase. They told me they did not see color, while somehow seeing mine clearly enough to comment on it.

This is what America taught me: race is not simply about skin. It is about the stories people attach to skin before they know anything else about you. It is about which accents sound educated, which hairstyles look professional, and which bodies are treated as if they must explain themselves.

And still, this is not only a story about America. It is also a story about becoming. About learning what has been placed on you, what you have accepted to survive, and what you eventually choose to put down.
